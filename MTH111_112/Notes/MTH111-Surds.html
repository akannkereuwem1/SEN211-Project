SURDS

Surds are irrational numbers that cannot be expressed as a finite decimal or fraction. They are often expressed using roots, such as square roots, cube roots, and fourth roots. Types of Surds: Simple Surds: surds that involve only one root, such as √2 or ∛3 Compound Surds: surds that involve more than one root, such as √(2+√3) or ∛(4-√5) Mixed Surds: surds that involve a combination of rational and irrational numbers, such as 2+√3 or 3-∛2 Operations on Surds: Addition and Subtraction: surds can be added or subtracted by combining like terms, such as √2 + √2 = 2√2 Multiplication and Division: surds can be multiplied or divided by multiplying or dividing the numbers inside the roots, such as √2 × √3 = √(2×3) = √6 Rationalization: surds can be rationalized by multiplying the numerator and denominator by a conjugate, such as (√2 + √3) / (√2 - √3) Laws of Surds: Law of Indices: a^(m/n) = (a^m)^(1/n) = √(a^m) Law of Surds: √(ab) = √a × √b Examples: Simplify the expression: √(4+√16) Rationalize the denominator: (√2 + √3) / (√2 - √3) Evaluate the expression: (√2 × √3) / (√4 + √9) Tips for Working with Surds: Simplify surds by combining like terms and using the laws of surds. Rationalize denominators by multiplying by a conjugate. Use the laws of indices to simplify expressions involving surds.
